About the Film

In the summer of 2025, I completed a 39-minute feature film in about one month. Cast, locations, and cameras were all replaced by AI — produced entirely from a single laptop at home.

Runtime 39 min (feature-length)
Video & Audio 100% AI-generated
Budget ¥84,261 / ~$581
Crew 1 person (planning through editing)
Hardware NEC LAVIE / Core i3 / 8 GB RAM / No GPU

The genre is psychological suspense. A young journalist investigating a terrorist incident from a small apartment gradually finds herself consumed by the darkness of the human subconscious. It spans 17 sequences, 8 characters, 12 BGM tracks, and 24 sound effects.

The concept dates to 2017, when I watched Alex Garland's Ex Machina in high school. After eight years, I started production in the summer of 2025 as AI technology matured.

Challenges and Solutions

Completing a 39-minute film meant hitting numerous technical walls. Here are the three most critical.

Challenge 1: The Face Changed Every Time

What happened

AI image generators produce a different face every run, even with the same prompt. A film requires consistent identity — this was a fundamental blocker.

↑ The reference face. Every scene had to preserve this identity.

Before — No LoRA

A different person generated each time

After — With LoRA

Consistent face maintained

How I solved it

Whisk and ChatGPT image referencing drifted after a few images. Solution: generate 31 expressions with Gemini, then LoRA-train on Replicate's Flux1.dev. Five runs to find optimal parameters.

Full parameter logs from all 5 LoRA training runs and decision rationale are in the paid course. Details will be sent to the email address you registered.

Challenge 2: LoRA Only Produced "Clean" Images

What happened

LoRA preserved character consistency, but horror effects (face covered in dark liquid) became impossible. Even with "black paint" in the prompt, the model defaulted to clean outputs.

Before — LoRA Only

Dirt/damage effects wouldn't render

After — ChatGPT Image Gen

Visceral texture achieved

How I solved it

Root cause: training on 25 "clean" images biased the model. I used ChatGPT image gen to create dark-paint images directly, using terms like "organic mass of meat," "charred," "coagulated" for visceral texture.

Test results, full prompts, and the LoRA v1–v5 evolution log are in the paid course. Details will be sent to the email address you registered.

Challenge 3: Every Method Before LoRA Failed

What happened

Before LoRA, I tried character consistency via Google Whisk + Gemini. Hair and facial features varied every time — far below film quality.

Before — Whisk + Gemini

Face unstable across generations

After — With LoRA

Consistent character maintained

Methods that failed

Technical prompts ("85mm lens," etc.) made the AI focus on camera specs, reducing facial accuracy. Pasting images into Gemini chat beat Whisk but added watermarks. LoRA was the only viable long-term solution.

The Short-to-Long Challenge

AI video tools output max 10 seconds per clip. Building 39 minutes (2,340 sec) under this limit is the core challenge every AI filmmaker faces.

The Core Problem

Runway Gen-4 caps at 10 seconds. Its extend feature causes abrupt color/motion shifts. Naive concatenation breaks dialogue-visual sync.

Solution: Forward-Reverse Lip-Sync

Alternate a 10-sec "forward" clip with its reverse. For mostly-static indoor footage, the reverse is nearly imperceptible.

StepAction
1Generate a silent 10-sec "forward" clip in Runway
2Create a reversed version using the reverse function
3Produce dialogue + silence = 10 sec audio via Hailuo Audio
4Trim audio to exactly 10.0 sec in Clipchamp
5Upload 10-sec audio to Runway → lip-sync video
6Interleave forward → reverse in Premiere Pro

Result: 10 sec × 2 = 20 seconds per cycle. Repeating this, I achieved long takes of ~2 minutes with visual continuity and lip-sync intact.

Pre-Calculation Was Essential

Dialogue length, pauses, and emotional pacing had to be calculated precisely before production. I used SRT files for second-level timing design.

Hailuo Audio's <#2.9#> syntax inserts 2.9 sec of silence. "Silence + dialogue + silence = 10.0 sec" uploaded to Runway triggers lip movement only during speech.

Limitations exist: lighting changes make reversal slightly visible, and Runway doesn't always output exactly 10 sec. Not perfect, but the most practical long-form AI filmmaking method available.
